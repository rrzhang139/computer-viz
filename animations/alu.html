where we are: where the math finally happens

Every page so far built one capability. The adder learned to add; the bitwise gates learned to AND, OR, and XOR; the MUX learned to pick one wire out of many. None of them chose what to do — each just did its one thing. This page is where those capabilities get assembled into a single block that does any of them on demand.

That block is the ALU — the arithmetic-logic unit. It takes two operands A and B, a 2-bit operation select op, and produces one result Y. This is the culmination of the combinational tower: the 4-bit adder, the bitwise gate-arrays, and the MUX, wired into one thing that can be told what to compute. Below it is everything you built; above it is the instruction that drives it.

Inputs on the LEFT (A, B, op), result Y on the RIGHT. Toggle the A and B bits and the op bits and watch Y in the readout.

everything computes at once

Here is the part that surprises people. The four blocks on the left aren't waiting their turn — they all run simultaneously, on every cycle, on the same A and B. The 4-bit adder is computing A + B; the AND, OR, and XOR gate-arrays are each computing their bitwise result. Four answers, ready in parallel, whether you asked for them or not.

Set A and B to anything and you'll see all four blocks light up together. Hardware doesn't decide first and then compute — silicon is cheap and time is precious, so it computes everything, then selects. Click the adder to drill into /adder4.html with these operands loaded; it's the same ripple-carry chain you built, four full adders deep.

op is just an address into a MUX of answers

So four results exist at once — how does one of them become Y? With the primitive from the MUX page. The 2-bit op drives the select of a 4-to-1 MUX sitting on the right: 00 → A+B, 01 → AND, 10 → OR, 11 → XOR. Whichever the select points at flows through to Y; the other three are simply not routed — computed, then dropped on the floor.

This is the quiet payoff of the decoder-and-MUX you built two pages ago. "Which operation should I do?" turns out to be the exact same question as "which input should the MUX forward?" — an address. The opcode field of a machine instruction is, quite literally, a select line into a bank of pre-computed answers. Click the MUX to drill into /mux.html.

same operands, different op

Watch the select do its work without touching the data. Set A = 0011 (3) and B = 0001 (1). With op = 00, the MUX forwards the adder: Y = 0100 (4). Now flip op0 so op = 01 — Y = 0001, the bitwise AND of 0011 and 0001. Flip to 10 for OR (0011), 11 for XOR (0010).

The operands never moved. The adder kept computing 4 the whole time, ignored. All you changed was which answer the MUX let through. That single moving select is the difference between an add and an and instruction.

why this matters

This is where add x3, x1, x2 actually computes. The register file hands over the values in x1 and x2 as A and B, the instruction's opcode sets op, the ALU produces Y, and on the next clock edge that result is written back into x3. The ALU is the verb of every arithmetic and logic instruction in the machine.

Widen every wire to 32 bits and bolt on a few more blocks — a subtractor (the adder with B inverted and carry-in tied high, two's complement), a comparator, a shifter — and this is the literal RV32I ALU. Pair it with the register file (which holds the operands) and the program counter (which decides which instruction runs), and you are no longer looking at a primitive. You are looking at the working core of a CPU datapath.
