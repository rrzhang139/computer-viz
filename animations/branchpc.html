where we are: no black boxes left

Every block on this page is a component you already built, embedded live: two copies of the 4-bit adder, the 4-to-1 MUX, and the register. Hover any block to see its real internals; click it to descend — adder → full adder → half adder → NAND → transistors, or register → flip-flop → latch → NAND → transistors. Nothing here is a drawing of an idea. It is the idea.

A program counter is not a special kind of circuit. It is an ordinary register whose job is special: it holds the address of the next instruction, and some machinery around it computes what that address should become.

the register IS the PC

Hover reg: four flip-flops, the exact register page you built in the memory chapter. The two low bits are our program counter (the upper two sit at zero — a real machine uses all 32). Its stored value leaves on the address wires to instruction memory, and nothing about the register itself knows it is "the PC" — the job comes from the wiring around it.

Its clock is the machine's heartbeat: once per cycle, whatever is staged at its D inputs becomes the new address. Everything else on this page exists to decide what to stage there.

the +1 adder — and why not use the ALU?

Hover +1 adder: the real 4-bit ripple-carry adder, with its B input hardwired to the constant 0001 — so its output is always PC + 1, recomputed live as the PC loops back into its A input.

A fair question: the CPU already owns an adder — the ALU — so why build another one? Because both are needed in the same cycle: while the ALU computes the instruction's result, the PC must simultaneously compute its own next value. And adders are cheap — even the MOS 6502, a chip of only 3,510 transistors, spent some of them on dedicated PC-increment circuitry. Every real CPU makes this same trade.

the target adder — PC + imm, with sign extension

The second adder computes the branch future: PC + imm. Its A input is the same looped-back PC; its B input is the instruction's signed offset. Watch the pink wires: imm₁ — the sign bit — fans into all the upper B inputs. That copying is sign extension, exactly how a real RISC-V core widens a 12-bit branch offset to 32 bits: a negative offset must stay negative when it grows.

the MUX chooses; the clock commits

Both futures are always computed and always waiting at the MUX — the real 4-to-1 MUX page, used as a 2-way switch (spare inputs tied to 0). PCSrc selects which one reaches the register's door.

Click PCSrc and watch the MUX picks readout flip between the two futures. Nothing is stored yet — then press pulse CLK 0→1→0 and the register captures the chosen one. Combinational logic proposes; the clock edge disposes.

why this matters

This is the fetch-front of every real CPU: a register of flip-flops, a dedicated incrementer, a branch-target adder with sign extension, and a PC-source MUX steered by PCSrc = Branch AND Zero. Jumps, calls, and exceptions each add one more input to the same MUX — more futures, same switch.

And it is turtles all the way down, by construction: click into either adder or the register and keep descending. Four pages from here you are looking at individual transistors — and every layer in between is a page you have already read.
