where we are: the machine's only decision

Everything else in the CPU computes values. This little corner is the one place a decision is made: should the next instruction come from PC + 1, or from the branch target? The answer is a single wire called PCSrc, and it is built from just two gates.

The names here are the standard ones from computer-architecture courses (Patterson & Hennessy's single-cycle datapath): Branch is the control line the decoder raises for a branch opcode, Zero is the ALU's "the result was zero" flag, and PCSrc = Branch AND Zero selects the PC's source.

the Zero flag — how hardware asks "are these equal?"

Hardware never compares two numbers directly. It subtracts them and asks whether the result is zero: A − B = 0 exactly when A = B. Our data is one bit wide, and one-bit subtraction is XOR — so the ALU's XOR result is the difference, and the = 0 ? block is just a NOT gate. In a 32-bit ALU the same block is a wide NOR across all 32 result bits, and its output pin is labeled Zero right on the ALU.

Click result to flip the ALU result and watch the Zero readout invert: result 0 means the values were equal, so Zero is 1.

the AND gate — both conditions, or nothing

Being equal is not enough — most instructions are not branches, and their results are often zero. Wanting to branch is not enough either — a beq whose registers differ must fall through. So the two conditions meet at an AND gate: PCSrc fires only when the decoder said "this is a branch" and the ALU said "they were equal."

Turn on Branch while result is 0. Now both AND inputs are 1 and PCSrc goes to 1 — this is the only input combination that redirects the machine.

why this matters

That output wire runs back to the fetch stage and flips the PC-source MUX. Two gates decide whether the machine walks forward or leaps. Every if, while, and for in every program ultimately funnels through an AND gate exactly like this one.

And notice the cost of the trip: the decision needs the instruction fetched, decoded, registers read, and the ALU finished — the longest path on the whole datapath — before fetch knows where to look next. Pipelined CPUs refuse to wait, guess the answer in advance, and pay a penalty when they guess wrong. That story is called the control hazard, and it starts right here.
